Recovery & Repair Stack

What actually supports post-workout recovery — the science on reducing soreness, accelerating tissue repair, and improving sleep quality for adaptation.

Ingredients
4
Est. Cost
~$1.60/day
Goal
Recovery
TL;DR — Quick Answer

The recovery stack is creatine monohydrate (5g), omega-3 EPA+DHA (2–3g), magnesium glycinate (300–400mg before bed), and collagen peptides + vitamin C (10–15g collagen, 50mg C, 30–60 min pre-training). Creatine accelerates muscle glycogen replenishment. Omega-3 reduces exercise-induced inflammation markers. Magnesium supports overnight tissue repair and sleep quality. Collagen + vitamin C pre-training enhances connective tissue synthesis — particularly relevant for tendons, ligaments, and cartilage.

ℹ️ This stack is for informational purposes only. Always consult a healthcare provider before starting supplements, especially if you take medication or have a medical condition.

The Stack

1
Creatine Monohydrate
Strong Evidence

Beyond acute performance enhancement, creatine accelerates post-exercise phosphocreatine resynthesis and muscle glycogen replenishment — reducing the time needed between training sessions. In practical terms: you recover faster, train more frequently, and accumulate more volume. This is the recovery mechanism most creatine users don't know about.

Dose
5g/day
Timing
Post-workout with carbohydrates (insulin improves uptake)
Est. Cost
~$0.10/day
📦 Best creatine products 2026
2
Omega-3 (EPA + DHA)
Strong Evidence

EPA and DHA inhibit prostaglandin synthesis from arachidonic acid — directly reducing the inflammatory cascade triggered by muscle damage during training. Multiple RCTs demonstrate significant reductions in CK (creatine kinase), IL-6, and muscle soreness markers after 4–8 weeks of supplementation. 2,000–3,000mg EPA+DHA is the effective dose for anti-inflammatory effects.

Dose
2,000–3,000mg EPA+DHA
Timing
With any fat-containing meal
Est. Cost
~$0.25/day
3
Magnesium Glycinate
Strong Evidence

Magnesium is essential for overnight tissue repair — it facilitates muscle relaxation post-contraction, supports GH and IGF-1 secretion during sleep, and regulates cortisol. Multiple RCTs show magnesium supplementation improves sleep quality in deficient populations, and improved sleep directly increases muscle protein synthesis rates overnight (the Res et al. 2012 pre-sleep protein work applies here).

Dose
300–400mg before bed
Timing
1 hour before sleep
Est. Cost
~$0.20/day
4
Collagen Peptides + Vitamin C
Moderate Evidence

Collagen is the primary structural protein of tendons, ligaments, cartilage, and fascia. Hydrolysed collagen peptides with vitamin C (which is required for collagen synthesis) taken 30–60 minutes before exercise increases circulating amino acids in connective tissue during the training stimulus, enhancing collagen synthesis in tendons and ligaments by up to 65% vs placebo (Shaw et al. 2017). Critical for injury prevention and tendon health.

Dose
10–15g collagen + 50mg vitamin C
Timing
30–60 min before training (on an empty stomach)
Est. Cost
~$0.60/day
⚠️ Collagen timing is critical: The collagen + vitamin C combination MUST be taken 30–60 minutes before exercise to work. Taking it after training misses the synthesis window entirely. The training stimulus combined with elevated circulating collagen peptides is what drives connective tissue adaptation — not just taking it daily.

What About BCAAs?

BCAAs are widely marketed for recovery. The evidence: if you're consuming adequate total protein (1.6–2.2g/kg/day), BCAA supplements add no meaningful recovery benefit. The amino acids are already present in your diet. BCAAs are useful only in two situations: fasted training (no protein for 3+ hours) or extremely low-protein diets. For everyone else, they're an expensive and unnecessary addition to this stack.

Scientific References

1. Shaw G et al. (2017). Vitamin C-enriched gelatin supplementation before intermittent activity augments collagen synthesis. Am J Clin Nutr. 2. Smith GI et al. (2011). Dietary omega-3 fatty acid supplementation increases the rate of muscle protein synthesis. Am J Clin Nutr.
