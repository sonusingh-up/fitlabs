What Is Creatine?

Creatine (methyl guanidino-acetic acid) is a naturally occurring nitrogenous compound synthesised in the liver, kidneys, and pancreas from three amino acids: arginine, glycine, and methionine. A typical omnivore diet provides roughly 1–2g/day from red meat and fish; your body endogenously produces another 1–2g/day. Total body creatine pool in a 70kg individual: approximately 120–140g, with 95% stored in skeletal muscle as free creatine and phosphocreatine (PCr).

Vegetarians and vegans maintain 20–30% lower baseline muscle creatine stores than omnivores — meaning they experience larger relative performance responses to supplementation. This is one of the most consistent findings in creatine research.

💡
Not a steroid. Not a drug. Creatine is a natural compound found in food and produced by your own body daily. It is fully legal under WADA, IOC, NCAA, and every major sports governing body worldwide.

How It Works — The Full Mechanism

During maximal-intensity exercise lasting 1–30 seconds, the phosphocreatine (PCr) system is the primary ATP-regeneration pathway. The core reaction:

Phosphocreatine + ADP → Creatine + ATP  [creatine kinase]

This reaction is dramatically faster than glycolysis or oxidative phosphorylation — making PCr the critical rapid-response energy buffer during explosive efforts. Creatine supplementation raises muscle PCr stores by 20–40% above baseline, directly expanding this energy reserve. The practical result: more reps before failure, higher peak power output, and faster PCr resynthesis between sets (enabling greater total training volume).

Beyond the PCr System

  • Cell volumisation: Creatine draws water osmotically into muscle cells. Intracellular swelling activates mechanosensitive protein pathways (mTOR, IGF-1) that stimulate muscle protein synthesis independent of mechanical loading.
  • Satellite cell activation: Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training enhances satellite cell activity — the myogenic stem cells responsible for muscle fibre repair and hypertrophy.
  • Myosin heavy chain upregulation: Several studies show creatine may directly upregulate Type II myosin heavy chain expression, increasing the fast-twitch contractile protein content of muscle.
  • Glycogen storage: Co-ingestion with carbohydrates may modestly improve muscle glycogen loading via insulin-mediated mechanisms — potentially extending endurance capacity in glycogen-dependent sports.
  • Brain energy metabolism: The brain uses the PCr system for rapid ATP regeneration in neurons. Supplemental creatine crosses the blood-brain barrier (slowly) and increases brain phosphocreatine, explaining the cognitive benefits documented in research.

The Evidence — What's Actually Proven

Strength and Power Output

📊 Meta-Analysis · Rawson & Volek (2003)
22 studies · Creatine supplementation increased 1RM strength by an average of 8% vs placebo during resistance training.
Upper body strength gains were 2× greater with creatine vs training alone. Effect was consistent across trained and untrained subjects.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Lanhers et al. (2017)
22 RCTs · Creatine significantly improved lower body strength across all tested populations including elderly adults.
Effect sizes consistent regardless of training status. Effect size (Cohen's d): 0.45 — considered moderate-large for a supplement intervention.

Muscle Mass and Hypertrophy

Creatine increases lean mass through two sequential mechanisms:

  1. Acute intramuscular water retention (weeks 1–2): 1–3kg. Temporary. Reverses within 1–2 weeks of stopping. This is not fat gain — it is water stored inside muscle cells alongside phosphocreatine.
  2. Genuine hypertrophy (weeks 4–16+): The ability to perform more total training volume over time leads to greater muscle protein accretion. This is real, permanent lean mass — not water.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Lanhers et al. (2017)
Average lean mass gain from creatine: +1.37kg above training alone over 4–12 weeks.
For natural lifters where every meaningful gain in lean mass requires sustained effort, 1.37kg over a 12-week training block is highly significant.

High-Intensity and Repeated Sprint Performance

📊 Multiple RCTs · Repeated Sprint Protocols
Repeated sprint capacity (5–10 sprint sets) improved by 5–15% with creatine loading.
Effects strongest in repeated-sprint sports where PCr resynthesis between efforts is rate-limiting. Benefits for single maximal sprints are smaller but still measurable.

Cognitive Function

📊 Rae et al. (2003) · Oxford University
5g creatine/day for 6 weeks improved working memory (backward digit span) and intelligence test scores in healthy young adults vs placebo.
Double-blind, crossover design. Effect sizes modest but statistically significant, especially for tasks requiring rapid mental computation.
📊 McMorris et al. (2007) · Sleep Deprivation Study
Creatine supplementation significantly mitigated cognitive decline caused by 24 hours of sleep deprivation.
The creatine group maintained near-baseline cognitive performance on choice reaction time and mood scales after a full night without sleep. Highly relevant for shift workers, medical staff, and military personnel.

Dosage — Everything the Research Shows

Standard Protocol (Recommended for Most)

3–5g of creatine monohydrate daily — taken consistently, every day including rest days. This will fully saturate muscle creatine stores within 3–4 weeks. No complex timing. No cycling. No loading required. Larger individuals (>100kg) may benefit from the upper end (5g) or slightly above.

Loading Protocol (Optional — Faster Saturation)

If you want maximal saturation within 1 week — before a competition, strength test, or training camp:

  • Days 1–7: 20g/day split into 4 × 5g doses (reduces GI discomfort vs one 20g dose)
  • Day 8 onwards: 3–5g/day maintenance

Loading achieves full PCr saturation in 5–7 days vs 3–4 weeks with gradual dosing. The end-state is identical. Loading causes more acute water retention and GI discomfort but has no long-term advantage over gradual dosing.

⚠️
Cycling is not necessary. Early recommendations to cycle creatine on/off were based on theoretical tolerance concerns, not evidence. Multiple long-term studies show no down-regulation of creatine synthesis or receptor sensitivity with continuous supplementation. Stay on it year-round.

Safety Profile — 30+ Years of Data

🔬
Summary: Creatine monohydrate has the most extensive safety record of any sports supplement. Studies up to 5 years, in athletes, healthy adults, children, and clinical patients all show no adverse effects in healthy individuals at recommended doses.

The Kidney Concern — Definitively Addressed

This myth originated from a single 1998 case report of a kidney patient who was advised to stop creatine. The confusion: creatine supplementation elevates serum creatinine — a metabolite used clinically to assess kidney function. In healthy people, this elevation is simply a reflection of higher creatine turnover, not kidney damage.

Multiple long-term controlled studies in healthy adults find no adverse effects on kidney function biomarkers beyond expected creatinine elevation. A 2021 systematic review of 24 studies confirmed this. For healthy adults, the kidney concern has no scientific basis. If you have pre-existing kidney disease, consult your physician before supplementing.

Documented Side Effects (Real but Manageable)

  • Water retention (1–3kg in week 1–2): Real. Intramuscular, not subcutaneous. Temporary. Reverses within 1–2 weeks of stopping. Not visible as bloating or puffiness in most people.
  • GI discomfort: At high loading doses (20g/day), some users experience bloating or loose stools. Resolved by splitting into smaller doses or using micronised creatine. Uncommon at standard 3–5g maintenance doses.
  • Muscle cramping (claimed, not confirmed): Multiple large prospective studies including NCAA athletes found no increase in cramping or musculoskeletal injury rates with creatine supplementation. This concern appears to be an anecdote rather than a documented effect.
