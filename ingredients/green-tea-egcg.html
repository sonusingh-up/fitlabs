What Is EGCG?

Green tea extract is standardised for its catechin content — a class of polyphenolic antioxidants. The primary and most studied catechin is epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG), which constitutes approximately 50–80% of total catechin content. A typical cup of green tea delivers 30–50mg EGCG; supplemental extracts are standardised to deliver 400–500mg per capsule — equivalent to 8–16 cups of green tea.

The COMT Inhibition Mechanism

EGCG's primary thermogenic mechanism is inhibition of catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT) — an enzyme that degrades catecholamines including norepinephrine (NE). By inhibiting COMT, EGCG extends the half-life of norepinephrine at adrenergic receptors — including β-3 receptors on fat cells that stimulate lipolysis (fat breakdown).

This mechanism explains why green tea extract works best when paired with caffeine: caffeine increases NE release; EGCG prevents its breakdown. The combination produces a significantly greater and more sustained increase in fat oxidation than either compound alone.

Evidence

📊 Dulloo et al. (1999) — Landmark Study
Green tea extract (containing 270mg EGCG + 150mg caffeine) increased 24-hour energy expenditure by 4% and fat oxidation significantly vs caffeine alone or placebo.
This seminal study established that the catechin-caffeine combination has effects beyond either compound alone — consistent with the COMT inhibition synergy mechanism.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Hursel et al. (2009) · 11 trials
Green tea catechins + caffeine significantly reduced body weight (−1.38 kg) and BMI vs caffeine alone over 12 weeks.
Effect sizes are modest but consistent. The realistic expectation: green tea extract supports fat loss but does not replace caloric deficit or exercise. It is a meaningful addition to a comprehensive fat-loss strategy, not a standalone solution.
📊 Venables et al. (2008) — Exercise Performance
Green tea extract supplementation for 4 weeks increased fat oxidation during moderate-intensity exercise by 17% vs placebo.
The fat oxidation increase during exercise suggests green tea extract may spare muscle glycogen — potentially enhancing endurance capacity beyond the simple thermogenic effect.

The Caffeine Synergy

The evidence overwhelmingly shows that EGCG works synergistically with caffeine for fat oxidation. Studies using EGCG alone (without caffeine) show much smaller or inconsistent effects. The recommended approach:

  • Take green tea extract standardised to 400–500mg EGCG
  • Combine with 100–200mg caffeine (from pre-workout, coffee, or supplemental caffeine)
  • Time before training for maximum thermogenic and fat-oxidation effect

Dosage

Target 400–500mg EGCG per day from standardised green tea extract. Read the label carefully: a product listing "500mg green tea extract" may contain only 50–100mg EGCG if poorly standardised. Look for "standardised to X% EGCG" — premium extracts are 80–95% EGCG.

Liver Safety — Important Warning

⚠️
Rare but documented hepatotoxicity: High-dose concentrated green tea extract has been associated with rare cases of liver injury, including liver failure requiring transplant. The risk appears confined to concentrated extracts at high doses (equivalent to >800–1000mg EGCG) and particularly in individuals with pre-existing liver vulnerabilities. At 400–500mg EGCG/day (food-equivalent doses), the risk is very low but not zero. Take with food, avoid combining with other potentially hepatotoxic supplements (kava, high-dose niacin), and stop use and consult a physician if you develop jaundice, dark urine, or upper right abdominal pain.
