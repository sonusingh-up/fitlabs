What Melatonin Is — And What It Isn't

Melatonin is a hormone produced by the pineal gland in direct response to darkness. Its primary biological role is as a circadian timing signal — it communicates to the body that it is night-time, initiating the physiological cascade associated with sleep preparation: core body temperature drop, reduced alertness, decreased metabolic rate.

This distinction is critical and widely misunderstood: melatonin is not a sedative. It does not directly cause sleep the way a sleeping pill or alcohol does. It shifts the timing of your sleep propensity — moving your sleep window earlier or later depending on when you take it. Many people take high-dose melatonin expecting a knock-out effect and are disappointed (or, concerningly, assume they need an even higher dose).

How It Works — The Circadian Mechanism

Melatonin acts primarily on two receptors: MT1 and MT2. MT1 receptor activation promotes sleep onset by reducing neuronal firing in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the master circadian clock. MT2 receptor activation shifts the phase of the circadian clock — moving your sleep-wake cycle earlier or later.

The key insight: melatonin administration sends a "darkness" signal to the SCN regardless of ambient light conditions. This is the mechanism behind its effectiveness for jet lag (re-synchronising the clock to a new time zone) and shift work adaptation.

Your pineal gland produces approximately 0.1–0.3mg of melatonin per night — a remarkably small amount. This is the physiological dose your body is designed to respond to.

The Dose Problem — Why 10mg Is Wrong

The majority of commercially available melatonin products are dosed at 5–10mg. This is 10–30× higher than the dose established as effective in the original research by MIT scientist Dr. Richard Wurtman — who literally discovered melatonin's sleep effects and holds the original melatonin use patents.

📊 Wurtman et al. (2001) · MIT — Foundational Dose Study
0.3mg melatonin was as effective as 1mg, 3mg, and 10mg for improving sleep onset. Higher doses were no more effective and produced supraphysiological blood levels.
Blood melatonin levels after 10mg supplementation can reach 3,000–6,000 pg/mL — compared to a natural nocturnal peak of 100–200 pg/mL. This 30-fold overshoot has no demonstrated sleep benefit and unknown long-term consequences for endogenous melatonin production.
⚠️
Why do high doses seem to work? At very high doses, melatonin may produce pharmacological sedation through off-target mechanisms — including effects on GABA-A receptors. This is distinct from the circadian timing mechanism. It works as a sedative, but this may mask the underlying circadian dysfunction rather than correcting it.

Evidence for Specific Uses

📊 Meta-Analysis · Ferracioli-Oda et al. (2013) · 19 RCTs
Melatonin significantly reduced sleep onset latency (by 7.06 min), increased total sleep time (by 8.25 min), and improved overall sleep quality vs placebo.
Modest but consistent effects across doses and populations. Effect on sleep onset was larger than effect on sleep maintenance. Greatest benefit in jet lag and delayed sleep phase disorder.
📊 Jet Lag · Cochrane Review (Herxheimer & Petrie)
Melatonin was consistently effective for jet lag across 10 RCTs, with optimal dose of 0.5–5mg taken at target bedtime at the destination.
Crossing more than 5 time zones eastward showed the largest benefit. Timing was more important than dose — melatonin taken at the wrong circadian phase can worsen jet lag.

Timing Guide

Melatonin timing is more important than dose. Taking it at the wrong time can phase-delay your circadian rhythm (making you sleep later) rather than phase-advance it (earlier sleep).

💡
If your melatonin causes morning grogginess, the dose is too high. Switch to 0.3–0.5mg. The half-life is 45–60 minutes — even 1mg should be cleared in 3–4 hours. Persistent next-day sedation at low doses may indicate slow CYP1A2 metabolism (a genetic variant affecting caffeine and melatonin processing).

Safety

Short-term melatonin use at low doses (<5mg) is considered safe in healthy adults. Long-term safety data is limited.

  • Endogenous suppression: Whether chronic high-dose melatonin suppresses the pineal gland's natural production is unclear — a theoretical concern that supports using the minimum effective dose
  • Drug interactions: Melatonin is metabolised by CYP1A2. Inhibitors (fluvoxamine, certain antibiotics) can dramatically increase melatonin blood levels. Inducers (heavy smoking) reduce them
  • Children: Some evidence for benefit in paediatric sleep disorders; use under medical guidance
  • Autoimmune conditions: Melatonin has immune-modulating properties — use with caution
