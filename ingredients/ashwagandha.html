What Is Ashwagandha?

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is a small shrub native to India, the Middle East, and parts of Africa. Its root has been used in Ayurvedic medicine for over 3,000 years as a "rasayana" (rejuvenating tonic). The modern evidence base focuses on the withanolide steroidal lactones — particularly withaferin A, withanolide D, and withanolide G — as the primary bioactive compounds.

Critically, the extract quality and withanolide concentration matters enormously. Raw ashwagandha root powder at equivalent weights contains far less standardised withanolide content than proprietary extracts. The majority of high-quality research uses KSM-66® (Ixoreal Biomed) or Sensoril® (Natreon Inc.) — two proprietary extracts standardised to defined withanolide percentages. If your supplement uses generic "ashwagandha root extract" without standardisation information, potency is unreliable.

How It Works — Mechanisms

Cortisol and HPA Axis Modulation

Ashwagandha's most robust mechanism is modulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis — the central stress response system. Withanolides appear to blunt cortisol release by reducing the sensitivity of the HPA axis to psychological and physical stressors, as well as potentially inhibiting cortisol synthesis via 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase.

Anti-inflammatory Pathways

Withaferin A has demonstrated inhibition of NF-κB signalling — a central inflammatory transcription factor. Reduced inflammatory signalling contributes to the recovery and anti-fatigue effects documented in athletic populations.

Testosterone and Reproductive Hormones

Cortisol and testosterone are inversely regulated at multiple levels. Chronic cortisol elevation suppresses GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) → LH → testosterone. By reducing cortisol, ashwagandha may indirectly improve testosterone in individuals where cortisol-mediated suppression is the rate-limiting factor. Some studies also suggest direct effects on LH signalling.

Thyroid Function

Limited but interesting data suggests ashwagandha may modestly increase T3 and T4 thyroid hormones — potentially contributing to the energy and metabolic improvements reported in some trials. Caution warranted in those with thyroid conditions.

The Evidence

Cortisol Reduction

📊 Chandrasekhar et al. (2012) · Double-Blind RCT · n=64
300mg KSM-66 twice daily for 60 days reduced serum cortisol by 27.9% vs placebo in chronically stressed adults.
Participants showed significant improvements on multiple validated stress assessment scales (PSS, GHQ-12, DASS). This remains one of the most cited ashwagandha studies due to its rigorous design.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Pratte et al. (2014)
Across 5 RCTs, ashwagandha consistently reduced self-reported stress and anxiety with a statistically significant effect.
Effect sizes ranged from moderate to large. Highest-quality studies (KSM-66/Sensoril, double-blind design) showed most consistent effects.

Testosterone and Body Composition

📊 Wankhede et al. (2015) · RCT · n=57 resistance-trained men
300mg KSM-66 twice daily for 8 weeks significantly increased serum testosterone (+15.4% above placebo), muscle strength, and muscle recovery compared to placebo during an 8-week resistance training programme.
1RM bench press and leg extension improved significantly more in the ashwagandha group. Muscle soreness recovery was also significantly faster. This study is frequently cited in fitness contexts — note the population were healthy young men with normal baseline testosterone.
💡
Important context: Testosterone effects appear most reliable in men with sub-optimal baseline testosterone (stress-related suppression, infertility). In men with already-optimal testosterone, the effect is likely smaller. Ashwagandha is not a testosterone replacement — it may restore suppressed levels toward normal.

Athletic Performance and VO2 Max

📊 Choudhary et al. (2015)
300mg KSM-66 twice daily for 8 weeks significantly improved VO2 max (+4.9%), time to exhaustion, and recovery time in healthy athletic adults.
Improvements in VO2 max of ~5% from a non-stimulant, non-blood-doping intervention is clinically meaningful. The study is a standout for ashwagandha's relevance to endurance athletes.

Sleep Quality

📊 Langade et al. (2019) · RCT · n=60
300mg root extract twice daily for 10 weeks significantly improved all sleep parameters including sleep onset latency (-19.7%), total sleep time, and sleep quality vs placebo.
Effects proposed to be mediated by triethylene glycol component (distinct from withanolides) and cortisol-lowering effects promoting sleep-compatible physiological state.

Dosage

The evidence-supported dose is 300–600mg of standardised extract per day, typically split into two doses. Look specifically for KSM-66® or Sensoril® on the label.

Who Benefits Most

  • High-stress individuals (work, training, life stress combined): Cortisol-lowering effects are most meaningful when baseline cortisol is elevated — common in hard-training athletes and high-stress professions
  • Men with sub-optimal testosterone from stress: Restoring cortisol-suppressed testosterone to normal range
  • Athletes focused on recovery: Reduced muscle damage markers and faster recovery demonstrated in controlled trials
  • Poor sleepers: Cortisol reduction + direct sleep improvement mechanisms make it one of the more evidence-backed natural sleep aids
  • Endurance athletes: VO2 max and time-to-exhaustion improvements are meaningful
⚠️
Limitations: Most testosterone and performance studies were conducted in stressed or sub-optimal populations. Effects in well-rested, low-stress athletes with optimal hormones may be significantly smaller than reported in published studies.

Safety and Contraindications

  • Generally well-tolerated at clinical doses. GI discomfort is the most common side effect (take with food)
  • Pregnancy: Contraindicated — potential abortifacient effects at high doses
  • Autoimmune conditions: Theoretical concern about immune stimulation — use with physician guidance
  • Thyroid conditions: May modulate thyroid hormone levels — monitor with physician
  • Liver concerns: Rare case reports of liver injury at very high doses — use within clinical ranges
