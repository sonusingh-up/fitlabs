What Is L-Citrulline?

L-Citrulline is a non-essential amino acid found naturally in watermelon (the name derives from Citrullus lanatus), cucumber, and various gourds. In the body, it undergoes conversion to L-arginine in the kidneys via the urea cycle — and arginine is the direct precursor to nitric oxide (NO), the signalling molecule responsible for vasodilation.

This is the core reason citrulline is in virtually every performance-oriented pre-workout: more citrulline → more arginine → more nitric oxide → more vasodilation → more blood flow to working muscles.

💡
Why not just take arginine directly? Oral arginine is extensively metabolised by the gut enzyme arginase and liver before reaching systemic circulation — first-pass extraction is 40–60%. Citrulline bypasses this entirely, converting to arginine in the kidneys with far greater efficiency. Multiple studies confirm citrulline raises blood arginine levels more effectively than arginine supplementation itself.

How It Works — Mechanism of Action

The citrulline → arginine → nitric oxide pathway is well-characterised:

  1. Intestinal absorption: L-Citrulline is absorbed across the small intestine with minimal first-pass degradation
  2. Renal conversion: Citrulline undergoes transamination in kidney tubules, converting to L-arginine
  3. Endothelial uptake: Arginine is taken up by endothelial cells lining blood vessels
  4. eNOS activation: Endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) converts arginine to nitric oxide
  5. Smooth muscle relaxation: NO diffuses into vascular smooth muscle, activating guanylate cyclase → cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation → vasodilation

The vasodilation produced by this pathway increases blood flow to contracting muscles — improving oxygen delivery, nutrient supply (glucose, amino acids), and the removal of metabolic waste products (lactate, hydrogen ions) that contribute to fatigue.

Beyond vasodilation, citrulline also participates in the urea cycle, facilitating ammonia clearance during exercise. Ammonia accumulation is a significant contributor to central fatigue — reducing it may extend time to exhaustion independently of the NO pathway.

Key Studies

📊 Pérez-Guisado & Jakeman (2010) — Landmark Study
8g Citrulline Malate before leg press increased total reps to failure by 52.92% vs placebo (p<0.01).
Double-blind crossover RCT, n=41. The same study found 40% reduction in muscle soreness at 24h and 48h post-workout. One of the largest single-ingredient effect sizes documented in sports nutrition.
📊 Bailey et al. (2015) — Blood Flow & Oxygen Kinetics
6g L-Citrulline supplementation significantly increased plasma arginine (+40%), reduced resting blood pressure, and improved oxygen kinetics during moderate exercise.
Crossover design, n=10 trained cyclists. Markers of vasodilation (flow-mediated dilation) were significantly elevated 90 min post-supplementation.
📊 Suzuki et al. (2016) — Resistance Training Performance
L-Citrulline (2.4g/day for 7 days) significantly improved mean power and peak power during explosive cycling efforts vs placebo.
Double-blind crossover, n=22. Lower doses show effects — reinforcing that citrulline is dose-dependent with meaningful benefit across a range.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Gough et al. (2021)
Citrulline supplementation significantly improved exercise performance vs placebo across 12 controlled studies, with larger effects at higher doses (6–8g).
Effect sizes were small-to-moderate (ES 0.17–0.42) but consistent. Authors noted dose-response relationship — underlining the importance of using clinical doses.

Dosage — Why Most Pre-Workouts Get This Wrong

The clinical dose from research is 6,000–8,000mg of pure L-Citrulline per serving. This is the dose range used in the studies showing the most dramatic performance effects.

The problem: the majority of pre-workouts on the market provide 3,000–4,000mg and present this as if it's sufficient. It isn't. While some effect likely occurs at lower doses, you are not getting the full benefit documented in the literature.

⚠️
The Citrulline Malate trap: Many products list "8g Citrulline Malate" — but in a standard 2:1 ratio (2 parts citrulline, 1 part malic acid), this provides only 5.3g of actual citrulline. A product needs to list ~12–16g Citrulline Malate 2:1 to deliver 8g actual citrulline. Always check whether the label shows pure L-Citrulline or the malate form.

Who Benefits Most

  • Strength athletes (≥6g pre-workout): Increased blood flow during compound lifts improves nutrient delivery and waste removal — more reps, faster recovery between sets
  • Endurance athletes: Oxygen kinetics improvement and ammonia clearance extend time to exhaustion
  • High-rep, high-volume training: The Pérez-Guisado leg press study showed the most dramatic benefit in volume-based protocols where acidosis and fatigue accumulate
  • Older adults with vascular issues: Blood pressure reduction and improved endothelial function make citrulline particularly valuable for cardiovascular health in this population
  • Those not wanting caffeine in pre-workout: Citrulline provides performance enhancement via a completely different (non-stimulant) mechanism

Buying Guide

  • Verify the label lists L-Citrulline at 6–8g per serving — not Citrulline Malate unless it's 12–16g of the malate form
  • Look for products with fully disclosed ingredient amounts — no prop blends that hide the citrulline dose
  • Pure L-Citrulline powder is inexpensive (~$0.30–$0.50 per 8g serving) — if you're paying much more, you're paying for branding
  • Timing: take 30–60 minutes before training, ideally on an empty or light stomach for best absorption
