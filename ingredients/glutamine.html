What Is L-Glutamine?

L-Glutamine is the most abundant free amino acid in the human body and the most concentrated amino acid in skeletal muscle (~60% of the free amino acid pool). Under normal conditions it is non-essential — your body synthesises enough from other amino acids. However, under conditions of extreme physiological stress (severe burns, post-surgical recovery, critical illness, very high training volumes), glutamine consumption by the immune system and intestinal cells can outpace synthesis, making it conditionally essential.

How It Works

Intestinal fuel source: Intestinal enterocytes (the cells lining your gut) use glutamine as their primary energy source — more than glucose. Adequate glutamine maintains the integrity of the intestinal barrier, preventing "leaky gut" phenomena where undigested food particles and bacteria can enter circulation.

Immune cell fuel: Lymphocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils all rely heavily on glutamine for rapid proliferation during an immune response. Exercise-induced glutamine depletion is associated with the "open window" of immune suppression seen in overtrained athletes.

Gluconeogenic precursor: Glutamine is a major gluconeogenic substrate — the liver converts it to glucose during extended fasting or under metabolic stress, sparing muscle glycogen.

GABA precursor: In the brain, glutamine converts to glutamate (excitatory neurotransmitter) and GABA (inhibitory) — potentially contributing to the anti-anxiety effects reported in some studies.

Glutamine for Muscle Building — The Honest Assessment

Glutamine supplements are heavily marketed for muscle building — and this is where the evidence is weakest. Studies measuring the effect of glutamine supplementation in addition to adequate protein intake on muscle mass consistently find little to no additional benefit.

The logic seems sound: glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in muscle. Supplement it and muscle grows. But this ignores that muscle protein synthesis is primarily driven by essential amino acids (particularly leucine) — and glutamine is non-essential. Your body regulates it independently. Adequate whey protein already provides ample glutamine.

Dosage

5–10g per day in divided doses. For gut health applications, research often uses 5g two to three times daily. For immune support during high training volumes, 5–10g total daily is the common protocol.

Who Genuinely Benefits

  • Athletes with recurrent upper respiratory infections: High training volume depletes glutamine and suppresses immunity — supplementation reduces this risk
  • Individuals with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or leaky gut: Strongest gut-health evidence; glutamine directly fuels the intestinal repair process
  • Post-surgical recovery: Clinical setting — glutamine IV or oral is established practice
  • Athletes during very high volume training blocks (>10 hours/week): Recovery support rather than direct muscle building
  • Vegans with low glutamine dietary intake: Animal foods are richer in glutamine — supplementation fills a genuine gap
