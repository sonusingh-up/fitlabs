How Caffeine Works

Caffeine's mechanism is structurally elegant. Adenosine — a nucleoside that accumulates in the brain throughout waking hours — progressively promotes sleep pressure by binding to A1 and A2A receptors. This is why you get increasingly tired as the day progresses and why you feel refreshed after sleep (adenosine clears during sleep).

Caffeine is structurally similar to adenosine and competitively antagonises its receptors — blocking the fatigue signal without removing the underlying adenosine. The consequence: reduced perception of effort and fatigue, increased alertness, faster reaction time, elevated mood, and improved sustained attention.

Secondary mechanisms include enhanced calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum in muscle fibres (increasing force production), increased catecholamine release (adrenaline, dopamine), and potentiation of β-adrenergic receptor signalling (increases glycogenolysis and fat oxidation).

💡
Caffeine doesn't eliminate fatigue — it delays the perception of it. Adenosine continues to accumulate during caffeinated performance. When caffeine clears, the accumulated adenosine signal hits suddenly — this is the mechanism behind the "caffeine crash."

Performance Evidence

Endurance Performance

This is where the evidence is strongest and most consistent. Meta-analyses consistently show meaningful improvements in time trial performance:

📊 Meta-Analysis · Doherty & Smith (2005)
Caffeine improved endurance performance by an average of 3.3% vs placebo across 21 studies.
For competitive athletes where margins of 1–2% can determine podium placement, a legal 3.3% improvement is remarkable. Effect was consistent across cycling, running, rowing, and swimming.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Grgic et al. (2020)
Caffeine supplementation significantly improved endurance exercise performance with an effect size of 0.36 across 46 trials.
Dose-response relationship confirmed — larger effects at 3–6mg/kg vs lower doses. No additional benefit above 6mg/kg, with increasing side effects.

Strength and Power

📊 Meta-Analysis · Grgic et al. (2018) — Strength
Caffeine significantly improved muscular strength (ES 0.19) and muscular endurance (ES 0.35) vs placebo.
Upper body strength showed larger improvements than lower body. Effect most consistent for bench press, less so for squat — potentially because upper body motor units have higher adenosine receptor density.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Grgic et al. (2019) — Power Output
Caffeine significantly improved power output (ES 0.17) — smaller than endurance but statistically robust.
Peak anaerobic power improvements averaging +6.5% in sprint-based protocols. Particularly relevant for team sport athletes, weightlifters, and combat sports.

Cognitive Performance

📊 Systematic Review · McLellan et al. (2016)
Even at 100–200mg (1–2 cups of coffee), caffeine reliably improves reaction time, sustained attention, vigilance, and working memory.
34 studies included. Effect was consistent in both sleep-deprived and well-rested individuals, though larger in sleep-deprived states. Training at a high technical level (complex movement skills, sport-specific decision making) also benefited.

Dosage by Bodyweight

The evidence-based dose is 3–6mg per kg bodyweight. Using bodyweight is more accurate than a flat dose because caffeine metabolism scales with body mass and lean tissue.

⚠️
More is not better above 6mg/kg. The dose-response curve flattens between 3–6mg/kg and reverses above. Higher doses increase anxiety, GI distress, heart palpitations, and performance impairment without additional ergogenic benefit. The sweet spot for most people is 3–5mg/kg.

Optimal Timing

Caffeine reaches peak plasma concentrations approximately 30–60 minutes after ingestion. For most people, taking caffeine 30–45 minutes before exercise aligns the performance-enhancing effects with training onset.

ScenarioTiming Recommendation
Morning trainingOn waking or 30–45 min before session
Afternoon training30–45 min before session; avoid after 14:00–15:00 if you sleep before midnight
Evening trainingUse with caution — caffeine's 5–6 hour half-life means 200mg at 19:00 still has ~100mg circulating at 00:00–01:00
CompetitionProtocol: abstain for 4–7 days pre-competition, then use at competition for maximal naive-receptor response
💡
"Sleep your coffee" timing: Some research suggests waiting 90–120 minutes after waking before consuming caffeine — allowing natural adenosine to clear first — leads to better sustained energy and less afternoon crash. This remains debated but has biological plausibility.

Tolerance, Cycling, and Withdrawal

Caffeine tolerance — upregulation of adenosine receptors and downregulation of caffeine's response — develops within 7–14 days of consistent daily use. This significantly blunts the ergogenic effects: habitual coffee drinkers who also use pre-workout caffeine often get less benefit than caffeine-naive users.

Tolerance Management Strategies

  • Abstention before competition: 4–7 days off caffeine restores receptor sensitivity. Studies show caffeine-naive users have 2–3× larger ergogenic response than habitual users.
  • Limit high-dose caffeine to training days: Maintain low caffeine on rest days (1 cup of coffee) and save high doses for training sessions.
  • Periodic breaks: 1–2 week caffeine breaks every 3 months can partially reset tolerance. Expect headaches in the first 24–48 hours — peak withdrawal typically occurs at 20–51 hours post-cessation.
⚠️
Caffeine withdrawal is real. Headache, irritability, fatigue, and difficulty concentrating are common symptoms 12–24 hours after stopping habitual use. These are temporary and typically resolve within 2–9 days. Gradual dose reduction (25% per week) minimises symptoms.

Safety and Contraindications

Safe for healthy adults at ≤400mg/day — the FDA, EFSA, and Health Canada all converge on this upper limit for healthy adults. At performance doses (200–400mg), the risk profile is low.

Contraindications and cautions:

  • Cardiovascular conditions: Arrhythmias, hypertension, structural heart disease — consult a physician before high-dose use
  • Anxiety disorders: Caffeine amplifies anxiety symptoms; dose conservatively or avoid
  • Pregnancy: <200mg/day — high intake associated with adverse foetal outcomes
  • Adolescents: No established safe upper limit; general caution advised
  • Medications: Potential interactions with certain antibiotics (ciprofloxacin), antidepressants (MAOIs), and stimulant medications
