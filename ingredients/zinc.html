Why Zinc Matters for Athletes

Zinc is an essential trace mineral involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body. For athletes specifically, the most critical roles include:

  • Testosterone synthesis: Zinc is required for luteinising hormone (LH) signalling and the direct conversion of cholesterol to testosterone via zinc-dependent enzymes. Deficiency causes measurable testosterone decline.
  • Protein synthesis: Zinc is a structural component of ribosomes and required for DNA/RNA polymerase activity — fundamental to all protein synthesis and cellular repair
  • Immune function: Zinc is essential for T-cell development and the activity of natural killer cells — the primary line of defence against infection
  • Insulin signalling: Zinc co-crystallises with insulin and is required for its storage in pancreatic beta cells — relevant for glucose handling and muscle glycogen management
  • Antioxidant defence: Zinc is a structural component of superoxide dismutase (SOD) — a major antioxidant enzyme

Athletes and Deficiency Risk

Athletes are at elevated risk for zinc deficiency for several reasons:

  • Sweat losses: Exercise-induced sweating can lose 0.5–1.5mg zinc per hour — significant over multiple sessions per week
  • Urinary excretion: Exercise increases urinary zinc excretion
  • High carbohydrate diets: Athletes following high-carb protocols often consume large amounts of phytate-rich grains and legumes — phytates bind zinc and dramatically reduce its bioavailability
  • Plant-based athletes: Animal foods have zinc in a much more bioavailable form — vegan/vegetarian athletes face higher deficiency risk
⚠️
Standard blood tests often miss zinc deficiency. Like magnesium, plasma zinc represents only a small fraction of total body zinc — it is tightly regulated and may appear "normal" even when intracellular zinc status is suboptimal. RBC zinc or hair mineral analysis provide better functional assessment.

Evidence

📊 Prasad et al. (1996) — Zinc Deficiency and Immune Function
Even mild zinc deficiency significantly reduced NK cell activity, IL-2 production, and thymulin activity — all key immune parameters.
Correcting zinc status restored immune function. This study established that even subclinical deficiency has measurable immune consequences — relevant for athletes in high-training periods with frequent upper respiratory infections.
📊 Kilic et al. (2010) — Wrestlers and Testosterone
4 weeks of zinc supplementation in exhaustion-induced wrestlers significantly raised testosterone levels that had been suppressed by intense training.
This study demonstrates zinc's role in maintaining testosterone under exercise-induced suppression — a real concern for high-volume athletes who are zinc-depleted.

Zinc and Testosterone — The Nuanced Picture

Zinc supplementation raises testosterone in zinc-deficient individuals — this is well-established. However, zinc supplementation in individuals who are already zinc-sufficient does not significantly raise testosterone above baseline. It restores, rather than supercharges, hormonal health.

The key question: are you actually deficient? Given the sweat-loss, phytate, and dietary factors above, many athletes are — but you cannot know without testing or taking a supplement history.

Take zinc with food to reduce GI discomfort. Avoid taking with phytate-rich foods (whole grains, legumes) at the same meal — eat them separately for maximum absorption.

The ZMA Protocol

ZMA (Zinc Monomethionine Aspartate + Magnesium Aspartate + B6) is a popular pre-sleep supplement based on a late-1990s study showing improved testosterone and IGF-1 in collegiate football players. Subsequent independent research has produced mixed results. Takeaway: ZMA is reasonable if you are deficient in both zinc and magnesium (common in athletes) — the benefit is primarily about correcting deficiency, not supraphysiological enhancement.

Toxicity and Upper Limits

The tolerable upper intake level is 40mg/day. Chronic intake above this level causes:

  • Copper deficiency: Zinc and copper compete for the same intestinal transporter (MT proteins). Excess zinc over time depletes copper, which can cause anaemia and neurological issues. If supplementing >25mg zinc daily long-term, consider 1–2mg copper supplementation.
  • GI irritation: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps — especially on an empty stomach
  • Reduced immune function paradox: Very high intakes (well above supplemental doses) impair immunity — the beneficial effect is U-shaped
