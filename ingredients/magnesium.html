Why Magnesium Matters for Athletes

Magnesium (Mg) is the fourth most abundant mineral in the human body and a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions — many of which are directly relevant to athletic performance and recovery:

  • ATP synthesis: All ATP (adenosine triphosphate) in the body exists as Mg-ATP complex. Without adequate magnesium, ATP cannot be used for energy. This makes magnesium essential for every single muscle contraction, neural impulse, and metabolic reaction that requires energy.
  • Muscle function: Calcium drives muscle contraction; magnesium drives muscle relaxation. The balance between these two minerals determines the resting tension in muscles. Low magnesium → impaired relaxation → increased susceptibility to cramping and spasms.
  • Protein synthesis: Magnesium is required for ribosomal function — the cellular machinery that assembles proteins. Inadequate magnesium directly impairs muscle protein synthesis efficiency.
  • DNA and RNA synthesis: Magnesium stabilises nucleic acid structures and is required for DNA replication and RNA transcription — fundamental to all cellular repair and growth processes.
  • Glucose metabolism: Multiple steps in glycolysis require magnesium-dependent enzymes. Low magnesium impairs glucose utilisation in muscle cells.

Forms of Magnesium — This Matters More Than Any Other Supplement

Unlike many supplements where form differences are minor, the form of magnesium you take determines almost entirely how much of it your body actually absorbs and uses. The most commonly sold form — magnesium oxide — has approximately 4% bioavailability. This means a 500mg magnesium oxide tablet delivers roughly 20mg of usable magnesium.

Evidence for Athletes

Sleep Quality

📊 Nielsen et al. (2010) · Magnesium and Sleep Quality
Magnesium supplementation significantly improved sleep quality parameters including sleep efficiency, sleep time, melatonin levels, and morning cortisol in older adults.
Mechanism: magnesium modulates GABA receptor activity (the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter system) — the same target as many sleep medications, but with a gentler, physiological mechanism.
📊 Abbasi et al. (2012) · RCT · Insomnia · n=46
500mg magnesium daily for 8 weeks significantly improved Insomnia Severity Index scores, sleep efficiency, sleep onset latency, and early morning awakening vs placebo in elderly adults with insomnia.
Double-blind design. Serum melatonin and renin also increased, suggesting magnesium's sleep benefit is at least partly mediated through melatonin pathway enhancement.

Testosterone

📊 Cinar et al. (2011) — Athletes and Sedentary Men
10mg/kg/day magnesium supplementation for 4 weeks significantly increased free testosterone in both athletes and sedentary men vs placebo.
The testosterone effect was larger in athletes — suggesting exercise combined with magnesium has additive effects on testosterone maintenance. Importantly, effect may be specific to correcting deficiency-related suppression rather than supraphysiological enhancement.

Muscle Function and Performance

📊 Reno et al. (2014) · Muscle Damage and Recovery
Magnesium supplementation significantly reduced markers of muscle damage (CK, LDH) following intense exercise compared to placebo.
The anti-inflammatory and calcium-balance effects of magnesium appear to reduce the extent of exercise-induced muscle fibre disruption, supporting faster recovery.

Cortisol and Stress

Magnesium deficiency amplifies the HPA axis response to stress — producing elevated and more prolonged cortisol release in response to stressors. This is a bidirectional relationship: stress increases urinary magnesium excretion (increasing deficiency risk), and deficiency amplifies the cortisol response to subsequent stress. Athletes in high-training loads are particularly vulnerable.

Dosage Guide

The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for magnesium is 310–420mg elemental magnesium daily, depending on age and sex. Most western diets provide 200–250mg/day — creating a typical shortfall of 100–200mg that supplementation can fill.

Note on elemental vs product weight: When a label says "Magnesium Threonate 2,000mg," the actual elemental magnesium is only ~8% = 160mg. This is not deceptive — threonate has special CNS-penetrating properties that justify the lower elemental content. Judge different forms by their elemental magnesium delivery AND bioavailability combined.

Signs of Magnesium Deficiency

Magnesium deficiency is notoriously difficult to diagnose because standard serum tests measure extracellular magnesium (~1% of total body magnesium). Intracellular magnesium (where 99% is stored) is not reflected in most blood tests. RBC magnesium or 24-hour urinary excretion are more accurate.

Common manifestations of suboptimal magnesium status:

  • Muscle cramps and spasms, particularly during exercise or at night
  • Poor sleep quality and difficulty staying asleep
  • Elevated anxiety and stress sensitivity
  • Fatigue and reduced exercise capacity (impaired ATP synthesis)
  • Increased PMS symptoms in women
  • Elevated resting blood pressure
  • Irregular heartbeat or palpitations (severe deficiency)

Risk factors for deficiency include: high carbohydrate/sugar intake, heavy alcohol consumption, high-intensity exercise (sweating increases urinary loss), type 2 diabetes, prolonged stress, and proton pump inhibitor (PPI) use.

What to Buy

  • Always check the form in parentheses — avoid anything listing "magnesium oxide" as the primary or only form
  • For sleep: look for Magtein® (Magnesium L-Threonate) — the patented form used in brain/sleep research. Standard product dose ~2,000mg delivering ~144mg elemental magnesium
  • For general supplementation and recovery: Magnesium Glycinate — high absorption, gentle on GI, widely available at reasonable prices
  • Target 200–400mg elemental magnesium from supplement to fill typical dietary gaps
