EPA vs DHA — The Critical Distinction

Not all omega-3s are equal. When people say "omega-3," they need to distinguish between:

  • ALA (alpha-linolenic acid): Plant-based omega-3 found in flaxseed, chia, walnuts. The "parent" omega-3 that must be converted to EPA/DHA. Conversion rate in humans: <5–10%. Not meaningfully interchangeable with EPA/DHA.
  • EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid): 20-carbon omega-3 with strong anti-inflammatory properties. Primary target for cardiovascular and inflammation benefits.
  • DHA (docosahexaenoic acid): 22-carbon omega-3. Predominates in brain tissue, retina, and sperm. Critical for brain development, cognitive function, and mood.

For athletic performance and general health, the targets are EPA and DHA combined — from marine sources or algae oil. ALA from flaxseed cannot substitute.

What the Evidence Shows

📊 REDUCE-IT Trial · NEJM (2019) · n=8,179
4g/day EPA (icosapentaenoic acid) reduced major adverse cardiovascular events by 25% in high-risk patients already on statins vs placebo.
One of the most significant cardiovascular trials of the decade. The high dose (4g/day EPA — far above typical supplement doses) produced dramatic risk reduction. This trial used pure EPA rather than the EPA+DHA combination in most supplements.
📊 Meta-Analysis · Calder (2015) · Inflammation
Omega-3 supplementation (EPA + DHA) significantly reduces serum inflammatory markers including IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP in a dose-dependent manner.
Effects most consistent at doses of ≥2g EPA+DHA/day. Athletes with high training-induced inflammation may benefit significantly — omega-3 reduces delayed onset muscle soreness duration and markers of exercise-induced inflammation.
📊 Depression Meta-Analysis · Mocking et al. (2016) · 13 RCTs
Omega-3 supplementation (primarily EPA) significantly reduced depressive symptoms vs placebo, with effect sizes comparable to antidepressant medications in mild-moderate depression.
EPA was the active component — DHA-dominant formulas showed less consistent antidepressant effect. Recommended alongside (not replacing) conventional treatment for clinical depression.

Dosage — Why 1 Capsule Isn't Enough

This is where most people get confused. A typical "1,000mg fish oil capsule" contains approximately 180mg EPA + 120mg DHA = 300mg total omega-3. The remaining ~700mg is other fats. To hit the 1,000–3,000mg EPA+DHA target, you need:

Purity and Sustainability

  • IFOS Certification (International Fish Oil Standards Program) — tests for PCBs, dioxins, heavy metals, and oxidation. Look for 5-star IFOS certification.
  • Keep refrigerated — omega-3s oxidise (go rancid) at room temperature, especially in capsules. Oxidised fish oil is less effective and may be harmful. Smell your fish oil — it should not be strongly fishy or rancid.
  • MSC Certification (Marine Stewardship Council) — ensures sustainable sourcing. Small, fast-reproducing fish (sardines, anchovies, mackerel) are more sustainable than large fish.

ALA — The Plant Omega-3 Problem

Flaxseed, chia, hemp, and walnuts contain ALA — the plant-based omega-3 precursor. The conversion of ALA → EPA → DHA in humans is extremely inefficient: <5–10% ALA converts to EPA, and <0.5% to DHA. This means plant-based omega-3 sources are NOT equivalent to marine sources for cardiovascular, brain, and anti-inflammatory benefits.

For vegans: algae oil is the solution. Fish get their omega-3 by eating algae — cutting out the fish and going directly to algae provides EPA and DHA with the same bioavailability as fish oil, without the sustainability concerns.
