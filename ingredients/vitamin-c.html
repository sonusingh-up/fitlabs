What Vitamin C Does

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is a water-soluble essential vitamin that humans cannot synthesise — unlike most mammals, we lack the enzyme L-gulonolactone oxidase, which is the final step in endogenous Vitamin C production. We are entirely dependent on dietary intake.

Its primary roles include:

  • Collagen synthesis: Vitamin C is an essential cofactor for prolyl and lysyl hydroxylase enzymes — required for the hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues in collagen. Without Vitamin C, collagen cannot form properly (the mechanism of scurvy).
  • Antioxidant: Directly scavenges reactive oxygen species (ROS); regenerates Vitamin E from its oxidised form; protects LDL cholesterol from oxidation
  • Immune function: Stimulates production and function of neutrophils, lymphocytes, and macrophages; accumulates in immune cells to concentrations 50–100× plasma levels; reduces duration and severity of upper respiratory tract infections
  • Iron absorption: Reduces ferric iron (Fe³⁺) to ferrous iron (Fe²⁺) — the absorbable form; dramatically increases non-haem (plant) iron absorption
  • Carnitine synthesis: Required for the biosynthesis of L-carnitine — essential for fatty acid transport into mitochondria

Evidence

📊 Cochrane Review · Hemilä & Chalker (2013) · 29 trials
Regular Vitamin C supplementation reduced the duration of colds by 8% in adults — and by 18% in athletes under heavy physical stress.
The athletic population shows a larger benefit, consistent with exercise-induced immune suppression creating higher Vitamin C demand. The "open window" of immune vulnerability after intense training appears to be partially attenuated by adequate Vitamin C status.
📊 Shaw et al. (2017) — Collagen and Tendon Health
15g collagen peptides + 48mg Vitamin C taken 60 minutes before exercise significantly increased collagen synthesis markers vs placebo.
The collagen-synthesis synergy: Vitamin C is required for collagen cross-linking — supplementing both together before activity that stresses tendons/ligaments may accelerate connective tissue repair and adaptation.

The Athlete Nuance — The Antioxidant Paradox

Here is the most important and counterintuitive aspect of Vitamin C for athletes: high-dose antioxidant supplementation immediately before or after training may blunt training adaptations.

Exercise produces ROS (free radicals) — which are required signalling molecules for adaptations like mitochondrial biogenesis, AMPK activation, and antioxidant enzyme upregulation. Flooding the system with exogenous antioxidants immediately post-exercise scavenges these ROS signals before they can stimulate adaptation.

📊 Ristow et al. (2009) — Antioxidants and Insulin Sensitivity
Vitamin C + E supplementation (1,000mg + 400 IU) prevented the exercise-induced improvements in insulin sensitivity and markers of mitochondrial biogenesis normally seen after 4 weeks of training.
Provocative finding — the antioxidants blunted the adaptations, not just the discomfort. This suggests that training-induced ROS are adaptive signals, not simply damage to neutralise.

Practical guidance: Don't take high-dose Vitamin C immediately before or after training sessions. Taking it at other times of day (breakfast, dinner) maintains systemic antioxidant protection without interfering with the peri-workout ROS signalling.

Vitamin C + Collagen for Tissue Repair

For athletes recovering from tendon, ligament, or connective tissue injuries — or simply wanting to support joint health — the combination of Vitamin C with collagen peptides has solid evidence. Take both 30–60 minutes before rehabilitation exercise or activity to support collagen synthesis in the target tissue while blood flow is enhanced to that area.

Dosage

The RDA (65–90mg/day) is sufficient to prevent deficiency. Research suggests the plasma saturation point is around 200–400mg — above this, excess is excreted in urine. For general health, 200–500mg/day from a combination of diet and supplementation is well-supported. For immune support during high-stress periods (heavy training, illness risk), 500–1,000mg/day is reasonable.

Iron Absorption Interaction

Consuming Vitamin C (100–200mg) with plant-based iron sources (spinach, lentils, fortified cereals) can increase non-haem iron absorption by 2–3×. This is particularly relevant for vegetarians and vegans who rely on non-haem iron and are at risk for iron deficiency. Simply drinking a glass of orange juice with iron-rich plant foods achieves this.
