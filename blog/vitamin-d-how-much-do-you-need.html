The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for Vitamin D is 600 IU/day for adults under 70 and 800 IU/day for those over 70. These figures were established by the Institute of Medicine in 2010 with a specific, conservative mandate: prevent deficiency diseases (rickets, osteomalacia) in healthy populations. They were not designed to optimise immune function, muscle performance, cardiovascular health, or hormonal balance.

This distinction matters enormously when evaluating what dose you personally need.

D2 vs D3: The Form Matters

Vitamin D comes in two supplemental forms: D2 (ergocalciferol, plant-derived) and D3 (cholecalciferol, animal-derived or lichen-sourced vegan). Research consistently shows D3 raises and maintains serum 25(OH)D levels more effectively than equivalent doses of D2. A 2012 meta-analysis by Tripkovic et al. found D3 supplementation produced approximately 87% higher serum 25(OH)D levels than equivalent D2 doses. Always choose D3.

What Dose to Take: Context-Dependent

Vitamin D dosing is not one-size-fits-all. The required dose to reach and maintain an optimal serum level (75-125 nmol/L) depends on your starting serum level, skin pigmentation, sun exposure, body weight, and intestinal absorption efficiency.

✅ General dosing framework (adults)
  • 2,000 IU/day maintenance: Sufficient for most adults with moderate sun exposure and no documented deficiency. Maintains most people in the 50-100 nmol/L range.
  • 4,000-5,000 IU/day: For those with documented deficiency, limited sun exposure, darker skin pigmentation, or over 55 years of age. EFSA considers this safe for all adults.
  • 600-800 IU/day (RDA): Inadequate for most people in northern latitudes or those with limited sun exposure — sufficient only to prevent overt deficiency, not to achieve optimal serum levels.
  • 10,000 IU/day maximum daily upper limit set by major health bodies — sustainable toxicity only emerges at sustained doses above this threshold over months.
☀️The India context: Despite proximity to the equator, an estimated 70-80% of Indians are Vitamin D deficient. Darker skin pigmentation requires 3-6× more sun exposure than lighter skin to produce equivalent Vitamin D. Additionally, urban lifestyles, covered clothing, and dietary insufficiency (Indian diets contain minimal Vitamin D) make sun-based correction essentially impossible for most people. 2,000-5,000 IU/day supplementation is appropriate for the vast majority of Indians.

Why Vitamin K2 Matters When Taking D3

Vitamin D3 at supplemental doses (>2,000 IU/day) upregulates calcium absorption and osteocalcin and matrix GLA protein (MGP) production. Both osteocalcin and MGP require Vitamin K2 (specifically the MK-7 form) to become functionally active. Under-carboxylated MGP in the absence of K2 may allow calcium to deposit in arterial walls rather than bones — the theoretical mechanism behind the K2 co-supplementation recommendation.

This is primarily a theoretical concern based on mechanistic data rather than definitive clinical trials showing arterial calcification from D3-without-K2 supplementation. However, the K2 co-supplementation is low-risk and mechanistically sound — take 90-200mcg Vitamin K2 (MK-7) daily when supplementing D3 at doses >2,000 IU.

Should You Test Your Levels?

A serum 25(OH)D test costs ₹300-600 at any major diagnostic lab in India (Dr. Lal, SRL, Apollo) or $25-60 in the US. Testing is recommended before starting high-dose supplementation (>5,000 IU/day) and annually for those on long-term supplementation protocols. Without testing, 2,000-4,000 IU/day is a conservative, safe starting point for most adults.

5,000 IU/day is within the safe range for adults. The Endocrine Society and most national health bodies set the tolerable upper intake level at 10,000 IU/day. At 5,000 IU/day, the vast majority of adults will achieve optimal serum levels (75-125 nmol/L) without approaching toxicity. Annual bloodwork is recommended for long-term use above 4,000 IU/day.
