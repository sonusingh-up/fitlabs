HMB (calcium beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate) was first characterised as a leucine metabolite in 1991 by Steven Nissen at Iowa State University. By the mid-1990s, Nissen's foundational studies showed HMB reduced muscle protein breakdown and increased lean mass versus placebo in both trained and untrained subjects. The supplement industry responded enthusiastically. Two decades of subsequent research has both supported and complicated those early findings.

What HMB Is — and What It Does

HMB is produced endogenously from approximately 5% of ingested leucine. The primary mechanism is inhibition of the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway (UPP) — the cellular degradation system responsible for breaking down muscle proteins. By suppressing UPP activity, HMB reduces muscle protein catabolism, particularly in situations where catabolism is elevated: caloric restriction, novel training stimuli, high training volume, and ageing-related anabolic resistance.

HMB also has a secondary pathway: direct mTOR activation for muscle protein synthesis. The anti-catabolic effect is larger and better-characterised; the anabolic contribution is modest compared to leucine itself.

🔬HMB works primarily by reducing muscle breakdown, not by dramatically increasing muscle building. Its utility is greatest when catabolism is the active problem — cutting phases, early training adaptations, and ageing. It is less useful when anabolism is the dominant goal in a healthy trained athlete at caloric surplus.

The pattern is clear: HMB shows consistent positive effects in untrained individuals, older adults, and users in caloric deficit. The effects in well-trained athletes at caloric maintenance or surplus are weak to absent. This is not a sign of poor supplement quality — it is the expected result from a compound whose mechanism (anti-catabolic suppression of protein breakdown) is only meaningful when protein breakdown is the active constraint.

Who Actually Benefits from HMB

✅ HMB Evidence Is Strongest For
  • Beginners (first 3-6 months of serious resistance training) — novelty of training stimulus creates elevated muscle damage and catabolism where HMB's UPP suppression is most impactful
  • Users in caloric deficit (cutting phases) — reduced energy availability elevates catabolism; HMB helps preserve lean mass during the deficit
  • Adults over 55 years — sarcopenic muscle loss involves chronically elevated UPP activity; long-term HMB intervention shows consistent lean mass preservation in multiple trials
  • High-volume athletes (multiple sessions/day) — recovery demand between sessions elevates catabolism; HMB may improve inter-session recovery quality

The Verdict

HMB is a legitimate anti-catabolic supplement for specific populations. It is not a muscle-builder for trained athletes in caloric surplus — that population has weak evidence and will be disappointed. It is a lean-mass-preserver for beginners, people cutting calories, and older adults — that population has consistent, multi-study support at 3g/day. Evaluate it for the population you are in, not the population the marketing copy assumes.
