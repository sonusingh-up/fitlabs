How We Ranked These — The Evidence Framework

We used three primary classification systems to rank supplements by evidence quality: the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) Position Stands, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) Supplement Classification (Categories A–D), and the broader peer-reviewed meta-analysis literature. Where these systems don't cover a supplement, we assessed the available RCT evidence directly.

Evidence TierWhat It MeansIOC Classification
Tier 1 — Strong EvidenceMultiple high-quality human RCTs, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses. ISSN or IOC endorsement. Consistent effects across diverse populations.Category A
Tier 2 — Moderate EvidenceSome human RCT data, but limited in quantity or with mixed results. Effects are real but smaller or more context-dependent.Category B or C
Tier 3 — Weak EvidencePrimarily in-vitro, animal, or single-study evidence. Theory-based claims without robust human RCT confirmation. Manufacturer-funded studies dominant.Category D or Unclassified

Tier 1 — Strong Evidence (Actually Work)

1. Creatine Monohydrate

IOC: Category A · ISSN: Strongly supported · Studies: 500+

The single most evidence-backed performance supplement in existence. Creatine monohydrate increases phosphocreatine stores in muscle, enhancing ATP regeneration during high-intensity efforts (0–15 seconds). Benefits: increased repetitions at a given load, increased power output, accelerated inter-set recovery, and lean mass gains of 1–2kg over 8–12 weeks in training studies. Additionally, emerging evidence supports cognitive benefits (Xu et al. 2024 meta-analysis: 16 RCTs, improved memory and processing speed).

Dose: 3–5g/day · Form: micronized monohydrate · Cost: $0.09–$0.48/serving

2. Protein Supplementation (Whey, Casein, Plant)

IOC: Category A · ISSN: Strongly supported · Studies: Hundreds

Not a single compound but a category: dietary protein above the general population RDA (0.8g/kg) significantly improves lean mass and strength outcomes when combined with resistance training. The target for muscle building: 1.6–2.2g/kg/day of total protein from all sources. Supplements become necessary when whole food sources cannot meet this target — which for most people is situational, not constant.

Whey protein is the most studied form — high leucine content, fastest absorption, most RCT evidence for MPS stimulation. Casein excels for pre-sleep use (slow-release, 8-hour amino acid profile). Plant proteins (pea + rice combination) produce equivalent outcomes to whey when total leucine content is matched.

Dose: 25–50g per serving · Form: whey, casein, or plant (your preference) · Cost: $0.50–$2.00/serving

Tier 2 — Moderate Evidence (Conditionally Worth It)

3. Beta-Alanine

IOC: Category A · ISSN: Supports · Studies: 50+

Beta-alanine increases muscle carnosine stores over weeks of daily use (3.2g minimum). Carnosine buffers muscle acid during high-intensity exercise, delaying fatigue and allowing more reps before "failure." The effect is most pronounced in efforts lasting 1–4 minutes — i.e., exactly the range of most resistance training sets and HIIT intervals. At the clinical dose (3.2–4g/day), it produces the characteristic paresthesia (skin tingling) for the first 2–3 weeks. The endurance benefit is real but modest — expect 2–3 additional reps at a given load, which compounds significantly over training months.

Dose: 3.2–4g/day (split into 2 doses to reduce tingling) · Cost: $0.30–$0.60/serving

4. Caffeine

IOC: Category A · ISSN: Strongly supported

Caffeine produces well-documented acute performance benefits: increased power output, reduced perceived exertion, improved alertness, and extended time to fatigue. The effect on resistance training is an indirect one — by allowing more total volume with better focus and reduced perceived effort, caffeine creates more total training stimulus over time. The dose: 3–6mg/kg of bodyweight, taken 30–60 minutes before training. Tolerance develops rapidly — using caffeine 3–4 days/week rather than daily preserves sensitivity. Most effective as pre-workout caffeine for trained individuals already comfortable with stimulants.

Dose: 3–6mg/kg (200–400mg for most adults) · Cost: pennies (cheap ingredient in any pre-workout)

5. Betaine Anhydrous

IOC: Category B · Moderate support

Betaine (trimethylglycine) at 2.5g/day has shown consistent but modest improvements in power output and training volume in several RCTs. The mechanism involves cellular osmolyte function and homocysteine metabolism. Effect sizes are smaller than creatine (roughly 30–40% of creatine's effect on strength), but the evidence is cleaner than most Tier 2 supplements. Worth including in a pre-workout or stack if budget allows. Not essential for beginners whose Tier 1 stack isn't yet dialled in.

Dose: 2.5g/day · Cost: $0.20–$0.40/serving

Tier 3 — Weak or Context-Dependent Evidence

  • HMB (Beta-Hydroxy-Beta-Methylbutyrate): Early studies (often industry-funded) showed impressive lean mass gains. Independent replications have been far less impressive. Some benefit in untrained populations; essentially no effect in trained athletes.
  • BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids): Beneficial when protein intake is inadequate (below 1.6g/kg). When total protein is already at the clinical target, BCAAs are redundant — you're supplementing amino acids already consumed in protein. Widely sold, widely overhyped.
  • Glutamine: Popular in the 1990s and 2000s. Multiple independent RCTs found no significant effect on muscle mass, strength, or recovery in healthy people consuming adequate protein. Still widely sold. Limited evidence of benefit.
  • Testosterone boosters: The broad category of herbal "T-boosters" (Tribulus terrestris, D-Aspartic Acid, fenugreek) has an extremely poor evidence base. No supplement has been shown to increase testosterone to a degree that produces measurable muscle or performance improvements in men with normal testosterone levels.

What Definitely Doesn't Work

⚠️ Save your money on: Most proprietary "muscle building" blends (underdosed by definition). Nitric oxide boosters containing ineffective forms of arginine. "Anabolic window" supplements that merely duplicate protein you could eat. Fat-burning muscle builders (a physiological contradiction under most conditions). Any supplement marketing "gains without training" — this is impossible.

Building Your Muscle Gain Stack — Priority Order

✅ FitLab Recommended Muscle Stack — 2026

1
Training programme first. No supplement produces muscle without a progressive resistance training stimulus. This is the prerequisite everything else depends on.
2
Total protein: 1.6–2.2g/kg/day. From whole food first. Supplement with whey, casein, or plant protein only to close the gap when food sources are insufficient.
3
Creatine monohydrate: 5g/day. One scoop of any NSF-certified micronized creatine monohydrate, every day including rest days. The single highest ROI supplement for strength and lean mass.
4
Pre-workout caffeine (optional): 200–400mg, 30–60 minutes before training, 3–4x per week. Either from black coffee or a clinical pre-workout like BULK Black or Legion Pulse.
5
Beta-alanine 3.2g/day (optional for high-volume training): Most useful for people doing HIIT, circuits, or high-rep sets. Less critical for pure strength training with long rest periods.
