The Bottom Line

Melatonin is one of the most commonly misused supplements in general use. Most commercial products — including many 5mg, 10mg, and 20mg options — are dramatically overdosed relative to the physiologically effective dose. Professor Richard Wurtman at MIT demonstrated that 0.3mg of exogenous melatonin achieves equivalent circadian phase-shifting to 3mg, with fewer next-morning side effects. Life Extension Melatonin 5mg is therefore 15-50x the minimum effective dose for healthy adults seeking modest sleep onset improvement.

That context stated, Life Extension is one of the most quality-controlled supplement manufacturers operating at scale — their own FDA-inspected GMP facility since 1980, and independent ConsumerLab testing confirmed their Melatonin 5mg is accurately labelled. At roughly $0.13 per dose for 60 capsules, this is one of the cheapest pharmaceutical-quality melatonin options available anywhere.

The recommendation depends entirely on use case. For jet lag recovery (5+ time zones), shift work sleep reversal, and adults over 55 with declining endogenous melatonin production — 5mg has good clinical backing and the Life Extension product is an excellent choice. For healthy adults under 40 seeking modest sleep onset improvement — 0.3-1mg achieves the same circadian effect with potentially fewer grogginess side effects.

The 5mg dose is high relative to the minimum effective dose (0.3mg) but matches the doses used in the strongest jet lag and shift work research. For the specific clinical applications this product is most appropriate for, the dose choice is defensible.

DOSING VERDICTScore: 7.2/10

Appropriate for specific use cases; excessive for general sleep onset

The minimum effective melatonin dose for circadian phase-shifting in healthy adults is 0.3-0.5mg — demonstrated by Wurtman's MIT group across multiple trials. The receptor system saturates at relatively low concentrations, meaning doses above 1mg do not produce proportionally stronger circadian effects. At 5mg, Life Extension delivers 10-17x the minimum effective dose for general sleep onset use.

However, 5mg is the dose used in the most positive jet lag studies (Herxheimer & Petrie 2002 Cochrane review), is appropriate for shift workers reversing sleep schedules, and is physiologically more appropriate for adults over 55 where endogenous production declines significantly with age. Context determines whether 5mg is correctly or incorrectly dosed for your specific situation.

The Cochrane systematic review by Herxheimer & Petrie (2002) found strong evidence for melatonin in jet lag prevention and treatment, with 5mg doses performing well. Mechanism — Chronobiotic, Not Sedative

Melatonin binds MT1 and MT2 receptors in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the brain's master circadian clock — signalling that environmental darkness has arrived. This triggers a cascade of circadian adjustments: reduced core body temperature, lowered cortisol, parasympathetic predominance, and initiation of the biological transition toward sleep. Critically, melatonin does not cause sedation in the way a benzodiazepine or antihistamine does. It creates the biological conditions favourable for natural sleep to occur. This is why melatonin works best when the circadian system is misaligned (jet lag, shift work) rather than for primary insomnia caused by hyperarousal or anxiety.

Key Evidence
  • Herxheimer & Petrie (2002), Cochrane Review — Systematic review of melatonin for jet lag. Found strong evidence for prevention and treatment of jet lag when taken at destination bedtime. 5mg doses well-represented in positive studies.
  • Brzezinski et al. (2005), Sleep Medicine Reviews — Meta-analysis of melatonin for sleep. Confirmed modest but significant sleep onset improvements. Dose of 0.3mg was as effective as higher doses for phase-shifting in healthy adults.
  • Arendt & Skene (2005), Sleep Medicine Reviews — Comprehensive review of melatonin as chronobiotic. Established the circadian phase-shifting mechanism and dose-response relationships.

Side Effects & Drug Interactions

⚠ Potential Side Effects
  • Next-morning grogginess — most common at 5mg; less common at 1-3mg
  • Vivid dreams — reported by some users, particularly at higher doses
  • Headache on first use — usually resolves; try taking with food
  • Mild nausea at high doses if taken fasted
  • Theoretical endogenous production suppression with long-term high-dose use — limited adult data; well-established concern in children at doses above 1mg
💊 Drug & Supplement Interactions
  • Blood thinners (warfarin) — melatonin may modestly affect coagulation; monitor if on anticoagulants
  • CYP1A2 enzyme substrates — melatonin is metabolised by CYP1A2; interaction possible with some medications
  • CNS sedatives / benzodiazepines — additive calming effects; generally manageable but be aware
  • SSRIs / antidepressants — generally safe; no clinically significant known interaction
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding — avoid supplemental melatonin; consult physician
